Hypertension (High Blood Pressure)

Overview

Hypertension, or high blood pressure, occurs when your blood pressure is 140/90 mmHg or higher. It is a common condition that can be serious if untreated, often presenting without symptoms. The only way to confirm hypertension is through regular blood pressure checks by a health professional or automated device, followed by professional evaluation to assess risk and related conditions.

Risk Factors

Factors that increase the risk of developing hypertension include:

  • Family history of hypertension
  • High salt intake, diet rich in saturated and trans fats, low intake of fruits and vegetables
  • Age over 65
  • Physical inactivity, overweight, or obesity
  • Tobacco use and excessive alcohol consumption
  • Co-existing conditions like diabetes or kidney disease
  • Environmental factors, such as air pollution

Diagnosis

Hypertension is diagnosed when systolic blood pressure is ≥140 mmHg or diastolic blood pressure is ≥90 mmHg (140/90) on two separate days. Regular blood pressure monitoring is essential for early detection, as many individuals experience no symptoms. A quick and painless measurement by a health professional is the most reliable method.

Symptoms

Most people with hypertension have no symptoms. However, very high blood pressure (≥180/120 mmHg) may cause:

  • Headaches
  • Blurred vision
  • Chest pain
  • Dizziness
  • Difficulty breathing
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Anxiety and confusion
  • Ringing in the ears
  • Nosebleeds
  • Abnormal heart rhythms

Regular blood pressure checks are critical, as symptoms may only appear in severe cases.

Treatment, Prevention & Control

The goal for most individuals is to maintain blood pressure below 140/90 mmHg. Lifestyle changes can significantly lower blood pressure, including:

  • Adopting a healthy, low-salt diet rich in vegetables and fruits
  • Quitting tobacco and reducing alcohol consumption
  • Increasing physical activity
  • Losing weight if overweight or obese

Some individuals may require medications, prescribed based on individual health conditions and doctor-recommended goals. Adherence to prescribed treatments is essential.

Complications

Uncontrolled hypertension can lead to serious health issues, including:

  • Heart damage
  • Kidney damage
  • Hardened arteries
  • Reduced blood and oxygen flow to the heart, causing chest pain
  • Stroke due to burst or blocked arteries in the brain

Regular blood pressure monitoring is key to reducing these risks and maintaining overall health.
