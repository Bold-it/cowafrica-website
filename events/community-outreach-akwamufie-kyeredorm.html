Community Health Outreach - Health Screening

Akwamufie & Kyeredorm, Eastern Region, Ghana November 21, 2020 Collaborators: Loveworld Church

A community health outreach initiative was conducted, focusing on the crucial task of testing for Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV), a virus that attacks the body's immune system; Hepatitis B, which is a serious liver infection caused by the hepatitis B virus (HBV); and Malaria, a life-threatening disease mostly found in the tropics, spread to humans by some types of mosquitoes.

The program aimed to reach underserved populations, particularly in remote areas where access to healthcare was limited. Integrated testing was offered, allowing for efficient use of resources and providing a comprehensive health assessment. The outreach, which was undertaken in two communities, Kyeredorm and Akwamufie, both in the Asuogyaman District, in the Eastern Region of Ghana, was funded by the Loveworld Church (formerly known as Christ Embassy Church), megachurch and Christian denomination. This outreach formed part of their Corporate Social Responsibilities, as well as Soul Winning event, as the church organized Gospel Crusades in both communities.

During the outreach, emphasis was placed on health education. Participants received information on disease prevention, transmission, and treatment. Counseling was provided on safe practices, promoting early detection, and addressing misconceptions and stigma. A critical component of the program involved linking individuals who tested positive to appropriate healthcare services for treatment and ongoing management, where referrals were made to the Volta River Authority Hospital.

Community involvement was key to the program's success. Community health workers and local leaders played a vital role in building trust and promoting participation. This community-based approach helped to ensure that the program was culturally sensitive and responsive to local needs.

The program, in the two communities, saw a total of 268 participants, with 96 males and 172 females. In the end, 5 persons (4 females and 1 male) tested positive for Hepatitis B; 2 persons (1 male, and 1 female) tested positive for HIV; and 34 persons, with most being children, tested positive for malaria. Since rapid test kits were used for the testing, the ‘Positives’ were counseled and referred to the VRA Hospital for confirmation and appropriate therapy. The results of the testing were handled with gross confidentiality, to prevent stigmatization, which is characteristic of rural folks.

The initiative had a significant impact on early disease detection, reducing transmission rates, and improving health outcomes. It contributed to achieving global health goals, such as the elimination of HIV, hepatitis B, and malaria. However, the program faced challenges, including limited resources, logistical difficulties in reaching remote areas, and addressing stigma and discrimination. Sustaining the program required ongoing funding, training of healthcare workers, and continued community engagement. Community health workers proved invaluable throughout the outreach. They provided education and bridged the gap between the healthcare facilities and the people they served.

A lot of appreciation to the Nurses and Laboratory Technicians from the VRA Hospital who volunteered in both communities: Faustina Gyamfua, Bertha Akorfa Agba, Awal Mohammed, and Nana Ama, as well as the Community Information Centers who availed their platforms for public announcements and information dissemination.
